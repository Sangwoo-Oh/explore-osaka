History

Osaka, once known as Naniwa, has been a vital center of Japanese culture and commerce for centuries. During the Edo period, Osaka flourished as a hub of trade and became known as the "Nation's Kitchen" due to its role in rice distribution.
The city played a significant role in the unification of Japan under Toyotomi Hideyoshi in the late 16th century. Osaka Castle, originally built in 1583, stands as a symbol of the city's storied past and enduring resilience. Today, Osaka continues to honor its history while embracing innovation and progress.

Year Era Description
645 AD Asuka Osaka's recorded history begins with the establishment of Naniwa as the capital of Japan during the Asuka period.
1583 Azuchi-Momoyama Toyotomi Hideyoshi completes Osaka Castle, marking the city's significance as a political and military center during the Azuchi-Momoyama period.
1615 Edo The Siege of Osaka concludes with Tokugawa forces defeating Toyotomi loyalists, solidifying Tokugawa rule over Japan.
1868 Meiji The Meiji Restoration brings about modernization in Japan, and Osaka emerges as an industrial powerhouse, contributing to the nation's economic growth.
1945 Showa Osaka suffers heavy damage during World War II bombings, but quickly rebuilds, showcasing resilience and determination.
1990s-Present Heisei, Reiwa Osaka continues to thrive as a vibrant metropolis, blending its rich history with modern innovations in commerce, culture, and technology.
